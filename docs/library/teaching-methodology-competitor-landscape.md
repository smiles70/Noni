## Part A: Five New Expert Sources — Teaching Methodology & Content Depth

These sources are **not** previously listed in `docs/library/README.md` or `digital-education-quality-resources.md`. They provide the academic grounding for *how* to teach deeply in a digital environment, complementing the geragogy sources that address *for whom*.

---

### A1. Sweller, van Merriënboer & Paas (2019) — "Cognitive Architecture and Instructional Design: 20 Years Later"

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article  
**Authors:** John Sweller, Jeroen J. G. van Merriënboer, Fred Paas  
**Journal:** *Educational Psychology Review*, 31, 261–292  
**Year:** 2019  
**URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-019-09465-5

**What it is**  
A comprehensive retrospective and update of Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) two decades after its formalization. Extends the original framework with new evidence on element interactivity, intrinsic/extraneous/germane load, and the implications for instructional design in digital environments.

**Key contributions for Noni**
- **Element interactivity** as the driver of intrinsic cognitive load — critical for pacing curriculum complexity.
- **Worked-example effect** and **expertise reversal effect** — justify the recap → principle → example → retrieval structure; worked examples reduce load for novices but can impede experts.
- **Collective working memory** in digital learning — distributed cognition between learner and interface.

**How it informs curriculum depth**  
The 4-page lesson structure (recap, principle, example, retrieval) directly implements the worked-example effect. Recap primes prior knowledge. Principle reduces element interactivity by stating the rule simply. Example provides a complete worked solution. Retrieval tests understanding with germane load only.

**How to cite**  
> Sweller, J., van Merriënboer, J. J. G., & Paas, F. (2019). Cognitive architecture and instructional design: 20 years later. *Educational Psychology Review*, *31*(2), 261–292. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-019-09465-5

### A2. Clark & Mayer (2016) — *e-Learning and the Science of Instruction*

**Type:** Academic textbook  
**Authors:** Ruth C. Clark, Richard E. Mayer  
**Publisher:** Wiley (4th Edition)  
**Year:** 2016  
**URL:** https://www.wiley.com/en-us/e-Learning+and+the+Science+of+Instruction%3A+Proven+Guidelines+for+Consumers+and+Designers+of+Multimedia+Learning%2C+4th+Edition-p-9781119239088

**What it is**  
The definitive practitioner-oriented synthesis of evidence-based guidelines for designing effective digital learning. Covers 25+ research-tested principles for multimedia, modality, contiguity, redundancy, coherence, personalization, segmenting, and pre-training.

**Key contributions for Noni**
- **Segmenting principle:** Break continuous content into learner-controlled segments — justifies the 4-page structure.
- **Pre-training principle:** Teach component concepts before the whole — justifies recap pages.
- **Personalization principle:** Conversational style enhances learning — supports Noni's "you decide" tone.
- **Coherence principle:** Eliminate extraneous words, pictures, and sounds — supports single-focus pages.

**How it informs curriculum depth**  
Each of the 25+ principles is a design decision that can be tested against curriculum content. For example: Does every page respect the coherence principle? Is the tone conversational (personalization) without being overly familiar? Is content segmented into learner-controlled chunks?

**How to cite**  
> Clark, R. C., & Mayer, R. E. (2016). *e-Learning and the science of instruction: Proven guidelines for consumers and designers of multimedia learning* (4th ed.). Wiley.

### A3. Merrill, M. D. (2002) — "First Principles of Instruction"

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article  
**Author:** M. David Merrill  
**Journal:** *Educational Technology Research and Development*, 50(3), 43–59  
**Year:** 2002  
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/BF02505024

**What it is**  
A unifying instructional design framework proposing five first principles that underlie all effective instruction: task-centered, activation, demonstration, application, and integration. Tested across multiple instructional theories and models.

**Key contributions for Noni**
- **Task-centered:** Learning is promoted when instruction is centered on real-world tasks — supports concrete scenarios.
- **Activation:** Learning is promoted when learners activate prior knowledge — supports recap pages.
- **Demonstration:** Learning is promoted when learners observe a demonstration — supports example pages.
- **Application:** Learning is promoted when learners apply knowledge and receive feedback — supports retrieval pages.
- **Integration:** Learning is promoted when learners integrate new knowledge into their lives — supports takeaway statements.

**How it informs curriculum depth**  
Merrill's framework validates the 4-page structure as a *task-centered instructional episode*. Each unit is a complete instructional event, not just content delivery. The framework can be used to audit whether any unit is missing a principle (e.g., a unit with no application/retrieval).

**How to cite**  
> Merrill, M. D. (2002). First principles of instruction. *Educational Technology Research and Development*, *50*(3), 43–59. https://doi.org/10.1007/BF02505024

### A4. van Merriënboer & Kirschner (2018) — *Ten Steps to Complex Learning*

**Type:** Academic textbook  
**Authors:** Jeroen J. G. van Merriënboer, Paul A. Kirschner  
**Publisher:** Routledge (3rd Edition)  
**Year:** 2018  
**URL:** https://www.routledge.com/Ten-Steps-to-Complex-Learning-A-Systematic-Approach-to-Four-Component/van-Merrienboor-Kirschner/p/book/9781138080806

**What it is**  
The comprehensive presentation of the **4C/ID model** (Four-Component Instructional Design) for teaching complex skills. Addresses whole-task sequencing, supportive information, procedural information, and part-task practice.

**Key contributions for Noni**
- **Whole-task sequencing:** Start with simple but complete versions of the whole task, then increase complexity — validates Module 1→5 progression.
- **Supportive information:** Information that helps learners reason about the task (principles, mental models) — maps to principle pages.
- **Procedural information:** Step-by-step guidance for task execution — maps to example pages.
- **Part-task practice:** Drill and practice for routine constituent skills — maps to retrieval pages.

**How it informs curriculum depth**  
The 4C/ID model provides a systematic way to assess whether the curriculum teaches *complex skills* (using Claude, building Skills, composing Agents) or merely *declarative knowledge*. Each module should be audit-able against the four components.

**How to cite**  
> van Merriënboer, J. J. G., & Kirschner, P. A. (2018). *Ten steps to complex learning: A systematic approach to four-component instructional design* (3rd ed.). Routledge.

### A5. Kalyuga (2012) — "Instructional Benefits of Attending to Worked Examples in Solving Problems"

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article  
**Author:** Slava Kalyuga  
**Journal:** *Educational Psychology*, 32(1), 1–12  
**Year:** 2012  
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1080/01443410.2011.583231

**What it is**  
A focused investigation of the worked-example effect — the finding that studying worked examples is more effective for novice learners than solving equivalent problems. Addresses the expertise reversal effect (worked examples become less effective as expertise grows).

**Key contributions for Noni**
- **Worked-example effect:** Novice learners learn more from studying complete examples than from independent problem-solving — validates the ExampleBlock structure.
- **Expertise reversal:** As learners gain expertise, worked examples can impede learning — justifies the complexity ramp (Module 1 is all examples, Module 5 requires more independent reasoning).
- **Efficiency metrics:** Instructional efficiency = (mental effort)⁻¹ × (test performance) — a quantitative way to measure curriculum quality.

**How it informs curriculum depth**  
The ExampleBlock is not merely a "story" — it is a *worked example* in the technical instructional-design sense. The learner studies the situation, observes what Claude does, and extracts the principle. This is more efficient than asking the learner to discover the principle independently. The complexity ramp ensures worked examples remain appropriate as expertise grows.

**How to cite**  
> Kalyuga, S. (2012). Instructional benefits of attending to worked examples in solving problems. *Educational Psychology*, *32*(1), 1–12. https://doi.org/10.1080/01443410.2011.583231

## Part B: Five Competitor Courses — Older Adult Digital Learning Landscape

These are real, operating courses/programs that serve older adults learning digital skills. They represent the competitive landscape against which Noni is positioned.

---

### B1. Senior Planet from AARP / OATS

**Organization:** Older Adults Technology Services (OATS), now part of AARP  
**URL:** https://seniorplanet.org / https://oats.org  
**Format:** Hybrid (in-person centers + live online classes + on-demand videos)  
**Cost:** Free (grant-funded and donor-supported)  
**Target:** Adults 60+

**Curriculum structure**
- Multi-week courses (typically 5–10 weeks, 1–2 sessions per week)
- Topics: tech basics, social media, telehealth, online banking, creative aging, AI literacy
- Structured progression: Basics → Intermediate → Advanced
- Instructors are trained older adults (peer model)
- Social component: classes include discussion, cohort bonding

**Strengths**
- Strong community and social element (C3 — social engagement)
- Peer instructors reduce intimidation (C5 — self-efficacy)
- Established national presence with physical centers
- Multilingual offerings (English, Spanish, Chinese)

**Weaknesses relative to Noni**
- **Not geragogy-first in design:** Classes follow traditional pedagogy, not a cognitively protective contract.
- **No systematic complexity ramp:** Learners self-select into classes without telemetry-gated progression.
- **Interface is standard video conferencing:** No cognitively protective UI. Zoom fatigue is real.
- **Content is broad, not deep:** Covers many topics superficially rather than one topic deeply.
- **No AI-specific curriculum:** While they have "AI literacy" sessions, there is no structured curriculum for Claude or AI assistants specifically.

**Strategic implication for Noni**  
Senior Planet owns the "community learning" segment. Noni should differentiate through depth (task-centered curriculum) and cognitive protection (predictable, low-load interface), not by competing on social features.

---

### B2. GCFLearnFree.org (Goodwill Community Foundation)

**Organization:** Goodwill Community Foundation, Inc.  
**URL:** https://edu.gcfglobal.org  
**Format:** Self-paced online tutorials (text + screenshots + short videos)  
**Cost:** Free  
**Target:** All ages; heavily used by older adults and adult learners

**Curriculum structure**
- Thousands of individual tutorials organized into topics (Tech Basics, Microsoft Office, Google, Social Media, Everyday Life)
- Each tutorial is a linear scroll of text + screenshots
- No formal progression or assessment
- Learners browse and self-select topics

**Strengths**
- Massive breadth of topics (C1 — comprehensive reference)
- Completely free, no account required for most content
- Text-first, screenshot-heavy format is low-bandwidth
- Self-paced allows learners to stop and return

**Weaknesses relative to Noni**
- **No geragogy design:** Dense text pages, small screenshots, standard web design (not age-optimized).
- **No instructional design framework:** Content is reference material, not structured learning. No recap, principle, example, retrieval.
- **No complexity management:** A learner can jump from "Mouse Basics" to "Excel Pivot Tables" with no guidance.
- **No AI curriculum:** Nothing on Claude, ChatGPT, or AI assistants.
- **Passive consumption:** No interactivity, retrieval, or application.

**Strategic implication for Noni**  
GCFLearnFree owns the "free reference" segment. Noni differentiates through structured, pedagogically-designed curriculum with built-in assessment and progression management.

---

### B3. AT&T Connected Learning / NCOA Digital Skills

**Organization:** AT&T Foundation + National Council on Aging (NCOA)  
**URL:** https://www.ncoa.org/page/connected-learning/  
**Format:** Online self-paced modules + in-person workshops at senior centers  
**Cost:** Free  
**Target:** Adults 50+

**Curriculum structure**
- Short online modules (10–20 minutes each)
- Topics: email, video calls, online safety, using smartphones
- In-person workshops at partner senior centers with AT&T-provided equipment
- Progress tracking through partner organizations

**Strengths**
- Corporate backing ensures sustainability and reach
- Partnership model leverages existing senior center infrastructure
- Equipment grants reduce access barriers
- NCOA credibility in aging services

**Weaknesses relative to Noni**
- **Very basic level:** Stops at email and video calls. No AI, no advanced concepts.
- **No curriculum depth:** Modules are single-session, not cumulative.
- **No cognitive protection:** Standard web modules, standard senior center classrooms.
- **No geragogy framework:** Content is functional skills training, not dignity-centered learning design.
- **No Claude/AI content:** Entirely absent.

**Strategic implication for Noni**  
AT&T/NCOA owns the "digital inclusion" segment (basic access). Noni serves learners who have passed this threshold and want to engage with AI meaningfully.

---

### B4. DigitalLearn.org (Public Library Association / Cox)

**Organization:** Public Library Association (PLA), funded by Cox Communications  
**URL:** https://www.digitallearn.org  
**Format:** Self-paced online modules (video + quiz)  
**Cost:** Free  
**Target:** All ages; public library patrons

**Curriculum structure**
- Short modules (15–30 minutes) on specific tasks
- Topics: computer basics, internet, email, Windows, Mac, mobile devices
- Each module ends with a short quiz
- Certificates of completion available
- Some modules available in Spanish

**Strengths**
- Library-based distribution reaches underserved populations
- Modular design allows targeted learning
- Quiz-based assessment provides feedback
- Free and accessible

**Weaknesses relative to Noni**
- **No geragogy contract:** Standard video+quiz format. Small text, no cognitive load management.
- **No complexity ramp:** Modules are standalone; no progression from simple to complex.
- **No AI content:** Nothing on AI assistants, Claude, or generative AI.
- **Passive video consumption:** Learners watch videos; limited interactivity.
- **Quiz framing is evaluative:** "Did you pass?" rather than "What did you learn?"

**Strategic implication for Noni**  
DigitalLearn owns the "public library basic skills" segment. Noni differentiates through AI-specific depth, geragogy-centered design, and cognitively protective UI.

---

### B5. GetSetUp

**Organization:** GetSetUp, Inc.  
**URL:** https://www.getsetup.io  
**Format:** Live interactive video classes (two-way video)  
**Cost:** Freemium (free tier + paid subscription for unlimited classes)  
**Target:** Adults 55+

**Curriculum structure**
- Live classes taught by older adult "Guides" (peer instructors)
- Two-way video: learners can ask questions, share screens, interact
- Topics: tech basics, smartphones, social media, health & wellness, hobbies, travel, finance
- Class sizes typically 10–20 learners
- Class schedule published weekly; learners book slots

**Strengths**
- **Live interaction:** Real-time Q&A, screen sharing, peer support (C3 — social engagement)
- **Peer instructors:** Guides are older adults themselves (C5 — relatability, self-efficacy)
- **Wide topic range:** Not just tech — includes hobbies, travel, wellness
- **Community feel:** Regular learners form cohorts and friendships
- **Higher engagement:** Live format creates commitment and accountability

**Weaknesses relative to Noni**
- **Not structured curriculum:** Classes are standalone sessions, not progressive modules.
- **No geragogy UI:** Uses standard Zoom-style video interface. Cognitive load is high (managing video, chat, screen sharing simultaneously).
- **No complexity management:** Learners self-select classes with no guidance on readiness.
- **No AI-specific depth:** While they have classes on ChatGPT, they are one-off sessions, not curriculum.
- **Requires synchronous attendance:** Missed classes cannot be made up easily.
- **Paid tier creates friction:** Freemium model may exclude learners who cannot afford subscriptions.

**Strategic implication for Noni**  
GetSetUp owns the "live interactive learning" segment. Noni should not try to replicate live interaction. Instead, Noni differentiates through: (a) structured, progressive curriculum, (b) geragogy-first UI design, (c) asynchronous flexibility, and (d) AI-specific depth that no competitor offers.

## Part C: Strategic Summary — Noni's Differentiation

| Competitor | Segment | Noni's Differentiation |
|:-----------|:--------|:-----------------------|
| **Senior Planet** | Community learning (in-person + online) | Depth over breadth; cognitively protective UI; telemetry-gated progression |
| **GCFLearnFree** | Free reference tutorials | Structured instructional design; geragogy contract; AI-specific curriculum |
| **AT&T/NCOA** | Digital inclusion (basic access) | Serves learners past the threshold; AI literacy; dignity-centered design |
| **DigitalLearn** | Public library basic skills | AI-specific depth; complexity ramp; confidence-preserving assessment |
| **GetSetUp** | Live interactive classes | Asynchronous flexibility; structured progression; cognitively protective interface |

### The Gap No Competitor Fills

None of the five competitors offer:
1. **A structured, progressive curriculum specifically for Claude/AI assistants** — all either lack AI content entirely or treat it as one-off sessions.
2. **A cognitively protective, geragogy-first UI design** — all use standard web or video interfaces.
3. **Telemetry-gated, complexity-managed progression** — all rely on learner self-selection.
4. **Confidence-preserving assessment (no scoring, no evaluative framing)** — all use traditional quiz/pass-fail models.

This is Noni's **defensible position**.
